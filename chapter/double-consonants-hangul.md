## Double Consonants

The last few consonants we need to learn to know all consonants of Hangul are the double consonants.

The following double consonants exist in Korean: ã„², ã„¸, ã…ƒ, ã…†, ã…‰

So these might already seem familiar. They look like their regular forms but doubled, written twice but still within the same space.
- ã„±: ã„²

- ã……: ã…†
- ã…ˆ: ã…‰

Double consonants are made with the same sound as regular consonants but by slightly tensing your mouth before pronouncing them. Hence they are also often called the tense consonants.

- ã„² sounds more like a "g", compared to "ã„±" which is more of a mix of between "k/g".
- ã„¸ sounds more like a "d", compared to "ã„·" which is more of a mix of between "d/t".
- ã…ƒ sounds more like a "b", compared to "ã…‚" which is more of a mix of between "b/p".
- ã…† sounds more like a sharp "s/z" sound, compared to "ã……" which is more like a regular "s".
- ã…‰ is harder to describe in romanized sounds. The official romanization for ã…ˆ is "j" and for ã…‰ it is "jj". Like described earlier, for ã…‰ pronounce ã…ˆ but with more force at the beginning of the pronunciation.
